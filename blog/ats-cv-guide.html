ATS-Proof Your CV: What Recruiters Actually Look For in 2025

March 25, 2026·7 min read·by AppTechno
📄

Over 75% of CVs are rejected by Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) before a human ever reads them. This is not hyperbole — it's the operational reality of recruiting at scale. Enterprise employers routinely receive 200–1,000 applications per role, and ATS software is the first filter applied to every single one.

The good news: beating ATS is not about gaming the system — it's about understanding what recruiters are actually looking for and expressing your experience in the vocabulary of the role.

What ATS Systems Actually Do

Modern ATS platforms (Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS) parse your CV into a structured record and score it against the job description using keyword matching, section recognition, and increasingly, semantic relevance scoring powered by AI. They are not fooled by keyword stuffing — they look for contextual relevance.

The parsing stage is where many CVs fail. If the system can't identify your name, contact details, job history, and education in the expected structure, you may score poorly regardless of your qualifications. Formatting matters — not aesthetics, but machine readability.

The Five Most Common ATS Rejection Reasons

  • Non-standard section headers: "My Journey" instead of "Experience." ATS systems look for standard headers like Work Experience, Education, Skills.
  • Tables and columns: Many ATS parsers read left to right across columns, scrambling the text and making it uninterpretable.
  • Missing job title keywords: If the role is "Senior Product Manager" and your CV never uses those exact words, you'll score poorly even if your background is a perfect match.
  • Inconsistent date formatting: "Jan 2022 – Present" vs "01/2022 – Current" confuses parsers and can cause your experience to be misclassified.
  • Skills buried in prose: ATS systems look for a dedicated Skills section — skills mentioned only in bullet points under job descriptions may not be extracted as searchable skills.

The Right Approach: Tailor, Don't Template

The single most effective ATS strategy is to tailor your CV for each application. This means:

  1. Read the job description carefully and identify the 10–15 most important keywords
  2. Ensure those keywords appear in your CV — in context, not as a list
  3. Match the seniority language used in the job posting
  4. Mirror the exact job title in your most recent position header if it's a reasonable match
  5. Adjust your professional summary to speak directly to the requirements of this specific role

Formatting Rules That Every CV Should Follow

Beyond keyword strategy, certain formatting choices dramatically affect ATS performance:

  • Use a single-column layout — Multi-column CVs are frequently misread by ATS parsers.
  • Stick to standard fonts — Arial, Calibri, Georgia. Decorative fonts may not render correctly in ATS parsers.
  • Save as PDF or DOCX — Both are widely supported. Avoid unusual formats like pages or Publisher.
  • No headers or footers — Many parsers skip header/footer content, so your contact details may not be captured.
  • Spell out acronyms at least once — "Artificial Intelligence (AI)" ensures both the full form and acronym are searchable.
  • Include a dedicated Skills section — List technical skills, tools, languages, and certifications explicitly.

Understanding Semantic Matching

The latest generation of ATS (and the AI screening tools increasingly used alongside them) don't just match keywords — they match concepts. A CV that says "built and maintained cloud infrastructure using AWS" scores for "cloud computing" even if those exact words don't appear. This means overly keyword-stuffed CVs may actually score worse than naturally written ones that demonstrate genuine expertise.

The practical implication: write about what you actually did, using natural professional language. The keywords will follow. What you need to add deliberately are the missing specific terms — the exact tools, frameworks, certifications, and job titles that appear in the target job description.

The Cover Letter Equation

Many ATS platforms score cover letters separately from CVs. A strong cover letter that directly addresses the job requirements adds a second keyword pass and demonstrates genuine interest in the specific role. Generic cover letters often score lower than no cover letter at all.

The best CV strategy is the same as the best strategy for human readers: show you understand exactly what this role needs, and demonstrate clearly that you have it.
